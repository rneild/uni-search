# Coaching Reference Notes

Research-backed insights to inform how I conduct the interview, interpret
preferences, and advise throughout the process. Read alongside `interview-plan.md`.

Last updated: 2026-03-21
Sources: Stanford Challenge Success, Gallup-Purdue Index, Strada/Gallup, HEPI,
Times Higher Education, Advance HE, EdCarpet, Crimson Education, Princeton Review.

---

## 1. What Actually Predicts Student Satisfaction and Success

**The headline finding:** Fit beats prestige. Consistently and clearly.

The Stanford Challenge Success white paper (*"A Fit Over Rankings"*) synthesises
the most robust research:

- **Selectivity does not reliably predict learning, job satisfaction, or wellbeing.**
  Across the strongest studies, there is no significant relationship between a
  school's ranking and those three outcomes.
- **There is a modest financial premium to attending selective schools**, but it
  applies almost entirely to first-generation and underserved students — for
  already-advantaged students it largely disappears.
- **Individual characteristics — background, major, ambition, and engagement —
  explain more post-college variance than the institution itself.**

### The Six Gallup Experiences

The Gallup-Purdue Index surveyed 30,000+ US graduates and identified six concrete
experiences that predict engaged, thriving alumni:

1. A professor who made learning exciting
2. Professors who cared about students as people
3. A mentor who encouraged personal goals
4. A project that took multiple semesters to complete
5. An internship that applied classroom learning
6. Active extracurricular participation

**Only 3% of students experienced all six. Only 14% had the relational
experiences (1–3).** The coaching implication is critical: *where* she goes matters
less than whether she will actually pursue these things once there — and whether
the institution makes them accessible.

### Satisfaction data is sobering

- 40% of UK students report regretting their choice of what or where to study (HEPI)
- 51% of US postsecondary graduates say they would change institution, degree type,
  or major (Gallup/Strada)
- Only 51% of final-year students say they would re-enrol at the same college

The consistent finding: **low faculty contact, poor advising, and social isolation
are the strongest predictors of regret** — not institutional tier.

**Coaching implication:** When she's weighing a high-prestige school against a
better-fit school, name this directly. Don't let prestige do unexamined work.
The question to ask is: which school makes the Gallup six *structurally accessible*?

## 2. Common Student Mistakes — What to Watch For

### What students over-weight

- **Rankings and brand name.** Rankings measure inputs (SAT scores, spending per
  student, reputation surveys) — not teaching quality, advising, or wellbeing.
- **The feel of a single campus visit.** A sunny day, enthusiastic tour guide, and
  attractive facilities can dominate a decision that should be made on evidence.
- **Peer and family prestige pressure.** Students routinely choose to impress others
  at home, not to fit their own academic and social needs.
- **Location glamour** before thinking hard about whether the academic structure and
  social environment will actually work for them.

### What students under-weight

- **Actual cost.** 77% of graduates who took out loans regret not planning better
  for debt. Students focus on the admission decision, not the financial decision.
- **Programme fit, not just university fit.** In the UK system especially, the
  specific degree course is the commitment. A student choosing Chemistry at a
  Russell Group research university is making a very different choice than Chemistry
  at a teaching-focused university — the institution's overall ranking says little
  about which is better for her.
- **Teaching quality and faculty access.** How large are first-year lectures? Will
  she have access to professors or mostly teaching assistants? These are almost never
  asked on prospectus days.
- **Career outcomes for their specific subject at that specific institution.**
  Overall graduate employment rates are misleading — a university with strong
  engineering outcomes may have weak humanities outcomes.
- **Mental health and wellbeing support.** 52% of UK students report their
  university does not provide adequate wellbeing services. Should be a criterion,
  not an afterthought.
- **Social-academic integration.** Students who feel isolated academically and
  socially are the most likely to disengage or transfer.

### The largest single regret: subject choice

- 36% of Americans would change their field of study — the most common regret,
  ahead of institution choice (28%)
- 52% of UK graduates regret their degree choice; most common reason: different
  choices would have opened more career options

**Coaching implication for UK applications:** Subject lock-in happens at age 17–18
in the UK system, and regret rates are high. This deserves substantial interview
time. If she's uncertain about her subject, that uncertainty should shape the
country-of-study decision before it shapes the institution list.

## 3. UK vs. USA/Canada — Differences That Actually Matter

### The consequential structural difference

| Dimension | UK | USA / Canada |
|---|---|---|
| Degree length | 3 years (4 in Scotland / integrated masters) | 4 years |
| Subject commitment | Fixed on application at 17–18 | Declared year 2; easy to change |
| Breadth | Deep in one subject from day one | General education requirements year 1–2 |
| Changing course | Very limited (usually first semester only) | High; double majors and minors common |
| Contact hours | 8–15 hrs/week | 12–20 hrs/week; more structured |
| Assessment | Final exams dominate at many institutions | Continuous: GPA, essays, participation |
| Independent study | Expected to fill most of the week yourself | More scaffolded week structure |

**If she is uncertain about her subject, or expects to change her mind, the
US/Canada system is structurally safer.** Half of US students change their major
at least once — the system is built for it. The UK is not.

### Daily life differences that surprise students

- **UK independence arrives fast.** 8–15 contact hours per week means students
  organise large amounts of self-directed study. Students who thrived in structured
  school environments sometimes struggle. Flip side: more autonomy earlier.
- **US campus life is more total.** Students eat, sleep, socialise, and study on
  or near campus. The residential, extracurricular model is immersive in a way UK
  universities typically are not.
- **UK student housing.** Halls typically in year 1 only; private renting from year
  2. Students cook for themselves — much more like adult life than US halls.
- **UK terms are short.** Typically 8–10 teaching weeks per term, 3 terms per year.
  More vacation but more concentrated exam pressure.
- **US campus sport is culturally central** in a way that has no real UK equivalent.
  If sport is part of her identity, this matters.

### Career and post-study — the under-discussed factors

| Dimension | UK | USA | Canada |
|---|---|---|---|
| Post-study work | 2-year Graduate Route visa | 12-month OPT (36 months for STEM) | Up to 3-year PGWP; most immigration-friendly |
| Employer recognition | Strong at top UK schools; weaker outside Russell Group | Well understood domestically; some US employers don't understand UK tiers | Generally well understood in North America |
| Co-op programmes | Rare | Less common than Canada | Structured, paid co-op terms common in engineering, CS, business |
| US grad school pipeline | Some structural disadvantage (grading, transcript, shorter degree) | Natural pipeline | Generally good |
| Medicine / law | Undergraduate entry — faster and cheaper | Graduate entry only — adds 4 years | Mostly graduate entry |

**Canada is significantly underrated.** Cheaper than the US, more immigration-friendly,
co-op programmes give real work experience before graduation, and post-study work
rights are better. Worth including in the longlist if not already there.

### Application process — two different registers

| | UK (UCAS) | USA (Common App) |
|---|---|---|
| Choices | 5 universities, 1 personal statement serves all | Up to 20, supplemental essays per school |
| Personal statement focus | Academic — "why are you qualified and passionate about this subject?" | Holistic — "who are you as a person?" |
| Recommendations | 1 referee (school) | Counsellor + 2 teachers |
| Deadlines | Oxbridge/Medicine: 15 Oct; most others: 31 Jan | Early Decision/Action: Nov 1–15; Regular: Jan 1–15 |

Students applying to both systems simultaneously need to write in **fundamentally
different registers** and plan carefully around different deadlines.

### Approximate annual costs for international students

| | UK | USA (private) | USA (public, out-of-state) | Canada |
|--|---|---|---|---|
| Tuition | £20–38k | $55–80k | $30–45k | $25–45k CAD |
| Living | £12–20k | $15–25k | $12–20k | $15–20k CAD |
| **Total** | **£32–58k** | **$70–105k** | **$45–65k** | **≈$40–65k CAD** |

Canada is significantly cheaper than the US for comparable institutions.

## 4. Financial Aid for International Students — Critical Points

**This is the area most likely to produce a nasty surprise. Surface it early.**

### Only a handful of US schools are truly generous to internationals

Schools that are **both** need-blind for international students **and** meet 100%
of demonstrated need (approximately — verify each cycle):
Harvard, Princeton, Yale, MIT, Dartmouth, Amherst, Bowdoin, Brown, Washington & Lee

Only ~12 US universities operate no-loan policies (replacing loans with grants).
Outside this group, "meeting full need" often still includes significant loans.

### Need-blind vs. need-aware — what it actually means

- **Need-blind:** financial need is not considered in the admission decision
- **Meeting full demonstrated need:** the school fills the gap between cost and
  what the family can pay — a *separate* commitment from being need-blind
- Most US universities are **need-aware for international students.** Needing aid
  can reduce admission chances, not just the aid package.
- At need-aware schools, a student requiring substantial aid competes in a smaller
  applicant pool with a smaller budget.

### How "demonstrated need" is calculated (and the traps)

- International students cannot use FAFSA. Need is assessed via the **CSS Profile**,
  which each school customises differently.
- The CSS Profile uses **Institutional Methodology**, which assesses assets FAFSA
  does not — home equity, family assets, sibling assets. This typically produces a
  **higher expected family contribution** than the federal calculation.
- There is no universal calculator. Two schools seeing identical financials may
  calculate very different awards.
- Aid packages must be recalculated each year; they can change.

### The merit aid tier — an underrated option

At elite universities (Ivies, MIT, Amherst), there is little to no merit aid —
all aid is need-based. If a student doesn't demonstrate financial need, they pay
full price regardless of academic excellence.

At a large tier of universities just below the very top (Tulane, Case Western,
Northeastern, University of Miami etc.), **merit scholarships are a pricing tool.**
High sticker prices ($70k+) but large merit awards ($25–40k/year) for desirable
applicants. For a family that wouldn't qualify for need-based aid but can't afford
full tuition, **this merit-aid tier may be more financially realistic than targeting
need-blind elite schools.**

Net price is what matters, not sticker price.

### Questions to ask every US university

1. Are you need-blind or need-aware for international applicants?
2. Do you meet 100% of demonstrated financial need for international students?
3. What percentage of the aid package is grants vs. loans vs. work-study?
4. How many international students received aid last year, and what was the average?
5. Is the aid renewable for all four years? What conditions apply (GPA etc.)?
6. Is there an appeal process if the package is insufficient?

## 5. Non-Obvious Questions to Ask Current Students

### Academic reality

- "How large are most of your first and second-year classes — taught by professors
  or teaching assistants?"
- "Have you ever had a one-on-one conversation with a professor outside office hours?
  How hard was it to arrange?"
- "Did you get into all the courses you wanted? Where were the bottlenecks?"
- "How much of your grade depends on the final exam vs. work throughout the year?"
- "What's the quality difference between the best and worst teaching you've had?"

### Support and advising

- "How useful was your academic advisor in year one, and how hard were they to access?"
- "If you were struggling in a course, what would actually happen? What support
  exists and did you use it?"
- "What do you wish someone had told you before starting here?"

### Daily life (the things prospectuses don't show)

- "What does a typical Tuesday look like for you?"
- "How do students actually socialise — within departments, year groups,
  accommodation, clubs?" (Reveals whether social life is structured or has to be
  created from scratch.)
- "What happens to social life here during reading weeks and out of term?" (UK
  campus empties fast.)
- "What does it actually cost you per month — not the university's estimate?"
- "If you needed urgent mental health support, what would you actually do and how
  long would it take?"

### Career and future

- "Did your department actively help you find internships, or was that entirely
  on you?"
- "How good is the alumni network for your specific subject, and have you used it?"
- "What percentage of your peers are going into the career they expected when
  they enrolled?"

### The meta-questions (most powerful, most rarely asked)

- "If you were starting over, would you come here again — and what would you do
  differently in year one?"
- "What would you change about this university if you could change one thing?"
- "What surprised you most — something you wouldn't have found in any prospectus?"
- "Is there anything I haven't asked that you think I should know?"

## 6. Red Flags to Watch For

Things that should trigger a closer look rather than automatic ruling-out:

- She names a university primarily by its name/ranking without being able to
  articulate what specifically draws her to it
- A UK university requiring direct entry to a specific course when she is uncertain
  about her subject
- US universities where financial aid for internationals is not clearly published
  — the lack of transparency is itself a signal
- Universities where student reviews consistently cite social isolation or a
  competitive/unhappy culture
- Any choice being made primarily to please a parent or match a peer group

## 7. Gaps to Listen for During the Interview

Things not explicit in the interview plan that are worth attending to:

- **How certain is she about her subject?** This may be the single most important
  question — it determines whether UK subject lock-in is a risk or a strength.
- **How does she learn best?** Exam-dominated vs. continuous assessment is a real
  preference that varies by person. Worth asking directly.
- **Has she spoken to anyone currently at these universities?** If not, flag as a
  research step before finalising any shortlist.
- **Is there a dream school she's reluctant to name?** Often there is. Give her
  space to say it.
- **How does her family feel?** Parental anxiety about distance, cost, or prestige
  often shapes the list more than the student admits. Worth naming gently.
- **Canada on the longlist?** It's often overlooked and offers real advantages
  (cost, co-op, immigration). Raise it if it hasn't been considered.
