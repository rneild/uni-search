# Coaching Reference Notes

Research-backed insights to inform how I conduct the interview, interpret
preferences, and advise throughout the process. Read alongside `interview-plan.md`.

Last updated: 2026-03-21

---

## 1. What Actually Predicts Student Satisfaction and Success

**The headline finding:** Fit beats prestige. Consistently.

- **Interest-major fit** is one of the strongest predictors of satisfaction,
  engagement, and completion. Students who study something they're genuinely
  interested in outperform students who chose a course for status or salary.
- **Person-environment fit** — whether the culture, pace, and style of the
  institution matches the person — predicts wellbeing more reliably than rankings.
- **Prestige is not a reliable predictor** of learning outcomes, job satisfaction,
  or wellbeing. It predicts some salary outcomes for some careers, but far less
  than commonly assumed.
- **Engagement matters more than where you attend.** Students who use office hours,
  join clubs, do research, and build relationships with faculty do better regardless
  of institution tier.
- **Teaching quality** is the factor most consistently associated with satisfaction
  in student surveys — more than facilities, location, or reputation.

**Coaching implication:** When she's choosing between a high-prestige school and a
school that fits her better, name this directly. Don't let prestige do unexamined
work in the decision.

## 2. Common Student Mistakes (Things to Watch For)

Research and surveys of graduates consistently surface these regrets:

| Mistake | How common / why it happens |
|---------|----------------------------|
| Chose the wrong subject | 37% of graduates wish they'd studied something different; often chose for salary or parental preference rather than genuine interest |
| Would have chosen a different school | ~27%; usually over-weighted prestige or ranking, under-weighted fit and culture |
| Under-researched the actual experience | Sampled only the official tour and intro lectures; didn't talk to real students or understand day-to-day life |
| Chose to please others | Parental preference, peer choices, or perceived expectations override her own read of what she wants |
| Over-weighted prestige | Name recognition drove the choice; didn't interrogate whether the teaching style, course structure, or culture suited her |
| Under-weighted finances | Cost was treated as a constraint to navigate rather than a major factor — then became a source of stress throughout the degree |
| Didn't use campus resources | Chose partly based on facilities, then didn't use them; underestimated importance of careers support |

**Coaching implication:** Watch for prestige bias operating silently. If she's ranking
a university highly but can't articulate why beyond "it's well-known," probe that.
Watch for "my parents want me to" as a stated reason — note it, but make sure her
own view is also on the record.

## 3. UK vs USA/Canada — Key Differences That Affect the Decision

These go beyond the surface. Each has real implications for her daily life and
future.

### Academic structure
| Dimension | UK | USA / Canada |
|-----------|-----|--------------|
| Specialisation | From day one — no changing your mind easily | Broad for 1–2 years; major declared in year 2 (US) or year 1/2 (Canada) |
| Degree length | 3 years (4 in Scotland / integrated masters) | 4 years typical |
| Contact hours | 8–16 hrs/week (lecture + seminar) | 12–20 hrs/week |
| Assessment | Heavily end-of-year exams (especially older universities) | Continuous assessment — essays, midterms, participation, finals throughout |
| Independent study expectation | Very high — UK degrees assume you fill the time yourself | More structured — US universities scaffold the week more |

**Key implication:** If she doesn't yet know what she wants to study, or thinks she
might change her mind, a US degree gives her far more room to do that. If she knows
exactly what she wants and wants depth fast, UK is efficient.

**If she's unsure about her subject**, this difference alone might resolve the
UK-vs-US question.

### Campus and social life
| Dimension | UK | USA / Canada |
|-----------|-----|--------------|
| Campus culture | Universities mostly city-integrated; students live in the city | All-encompassing campus culture, especially US; sports, Greek life, campus events central |
| Accommodation | Halls typically year 1 only; private rental after | On-campus housing often available all years |
| Social life | Organised around city nightlife and student unions | Organised around campus — Greek life, sports teams, clubs |

### Career and post-graduation
| Dimension | UK | USA / Canada |
|-----------|-----|--------------|
| Post-study work visa | UK Graduate Visa: 2 years to work freely | USA: OPT (1 year, up to 3 for STEM); requires employer sponsorship for longer |
| Employer recognition (UK degree, working in US) | Good at top UK schools; less understood at regional UK universities | N/A |
| Professional degrees | UK: direct entry to medicine, law, dentistry from undergraduate | USA: medicine and law are graduate-entry only (adds 4 years to training) |
| Graduate outcomes | Strong at LSE, Imperial, Warwick, Russell Group — sometimes under-rated globally | Strong at research universities; liberal arts outcomes well understood domestically |

**Key implication for international students:** If she might want to work in the US
after graduating, the OPT visa situation is important. STEM subjects get 3 years of
OPT, giving more time to secure sponsorship. Non-STEM is harder.

### Cost (annual, approximate for international students)
| | UK | USA (private) | USA (public, out-of-state) | Canada |
|--|-----|--------------|--------------------------|--------|
| Tuition | £20–38k | $55–80k | $30–45k | $25–45k CAD (~$18–33k USD) |
| Living | £12–20k (London higher) | $15–25k | $12–20k | $15–20k CAD |
| **Total** | **£32–58k** | **$70–105k** | **$45–65k** | **$40–65k CAD** |

Canada is significantly cheaper than the US for comparable institutions.

## 4. Financial Aid for International Students — Critical Points

**This is the area most likely to produce a nasty surprise.** Surface it early.

### The fundamental asymmetry
Most US universities are **need-aware for international students** — meaning your
financial need can count against your admission. Being honest about needing aid can
reduce your chances of getting in.

### Only a handful of schools are truly generous to internationals
**Need-blind for international students AND meet 100% of demonstrated need:**
Harvard, Princeton, Yale, MIT, Dartmouth, Amherst, Washington & Lee, Brown
(approximately — this list changes; verify for each application cycle).

Only ~12 US universities operate no-loan policies (they replace loans with grants).
Outside this group, "meeting full need" often still means significant loans.

### What this means practically
- If the family cannot afford $70–100k/year without significant aid, the target
  list for US universities must be built around schools that are both likely
  to admit her AND likely to give her substantial aid.
- These two things (selectivity + generosity to internationals) often coincide at
  the very top schools — but admission is also hardest there.
- **Canadian universities** are cheaper and some offer merit scholarships to
  international students with less complexity.
- **UK universities** have a simpler cost picture: published tuition fees, Student
  Finance not available to most internationals, but fewer surprises.

### Questions to ask every US university
1. Are you need-blind or need-aware for international applicants?
2. Do you meet 100% of demonstrated financial need for international students?
3. What percentage of your international students receive need-based aid?
4. What is the average aid package for international students?
5. Is your aid renewable for all four years, or just year one?

## 5. Questions to Ask Current Students (That They Typically Don't Think to Ask)

These are more revealing than official tour questions:

**Academic reality**
- How hard is it to get into the courses you actually want? (Registration battles)
- Is the teaching mostly by professors or by grad students / TAs?
- How good is academic advising? Do you actually feel supported?
- How collaborative vs competitive is the culture among students?

**Daily life**
- What do you actually eat, and is it bearable? (Food quality is a real quality-of-life issue)
- If you could change one thing about living here, what would it be?
- What's a typical Tuesday like for you?

**Social and culture**
- How did you make your close friends? (Reveals how open or cliquey the culture is)
- Does the university's stated culture match the actual culture?
- How do international students fit in — genuinely, not officially?
- What's the party culture actually like? (Often very different from the brochure)

**Career and future**
- Did the university's careers service actually help you find work/internships?
- Do employers know and respect this degree?

**The meta-question**
- Knowing what you know now, would you choose this university again?

## 6. Red Flags to Watch For (During Research and Interview)

Things that should trigger a closer look rather than an automatic ruling-out:

- She names a university primarily because of its name/ranking, without being able
  to say what specifically draws her to it
- A university that requires direct entry to a specific course when she's uncertain
  about her subject (UK system risk)
- US universities where financial aid for international students is not published
  clearly — the lack of transparency is itself a signal
- Universities where most current-student reviews cite social isolation or a
  competitive/unhappy culture
- Any choice being made primarily to please a parent or match a peer group

## 7. Gaps to Watch During the Interview

Things not in the interview plan that are worth listening for:

- **How certain is she about her subject?** This determines whether UK specialisation
  is a risk or a strength. If uncertain, weight US/Canada higher.
- **Does she have a sense of how she learns best?** Exam-focused vs continuous
  assessment is a real preference that varies person to person.
- **Has she talked to anyone currently at these universities?** If not, flag this
  as a research step before finalising any shortlist.
- **Is there a "dream school" she's reluctant to say out loud?** Often there is.
  Give her space to name it.
- **How does her family feel?** Parental anxiety about distance, cost, or prestige
  often shapes the list more than the student admits. Worth naming gently.
